The Co-Intelligent Enterprise

Why AI is HR's Responsibility

How HR leaders can move from AI anxiety to organisational advantage — and why this is your mandate, not IT's.

Keynote AI & HR Strategy HR Leaders & Executives 9 slides
What is Co-Intelligence?

A term coined by Professor Ethan Mollick to describe a future where humans and AI work together as active, smart collaborators — not where AI replaces humans, but where both are better together.

The Fundamental Shift

We are not discussing a new software rollout. We are talking about a fundamental shift in how we work. The goal: move from viewing AI as a "button" to viewing it as a teammate. This is an organisational design and human incentive problem — and that makes it HR's.

It's a Teammate, Not a Tool

Slide 2

Stop treating this as a tech implementation

Traditional software is predictable and follows strict rules. AI does not. It is erratic, prone to "hallucinations," and behaves more like an analytical partner or a "confused intern" — brilliant in flashes, unreliable in ways you cannot predict in advance.

"AI can pass the Bar Exam at the 90th percentile — and fail at basic word counts or simple logic puzzles. This is not a technology problem. It is a talent management challenge."
Show & Tell Moment

Aha! moment for the room: Ask the audience to guess how AI would score on the Bar Exam. Then reveal it passes at the 90th percentile. Then ask it to count the letter "r" in the word "strawberry" — and watch it fail. The contrast makes the point viscerally: AI is not a calculator. It is something altogether stranger.

The message: we must stop asking IT to manage this. We must start asking HR to lead it.

Mapping the Jagged Frontier

Slide 3
The Core Challenge

The wall is invisible — and constantly moving

Imagine a fortress wall. Inside the wall are tasks AI is brilliant at — generating 50 marketing slogans in seconds, drafting a policy template, summarising 200 pages of research. Outside the wall are tasks it fails at — high-stakes factual recall, nuanced ethical reasoning, counting letters in a word.

The problem is not where the wall sits. The problem is that no one can see it, and it moves outward every few months as models improve. There is no instruction manual.

HR Action

You cannot wait for a map to be handed to you. HR's role is to encourage every team to experiment — to push until they find where the wall lies for their specific roles. The organisation that explores fastest learns fastest.

Assign one person per department to spend 30 minutes a week deliberately testing AI on tasks they currently do manually. Have them report back monthly on what worked and what did not.

Hard Question
"The wall is invisible and constantly moving outward. How do you write a governance policy for something that changes every three months?"

The 4 Rules for Co-Intelligence

Slide 4

To navigate the Jagged Frontier, every employee — from the CEO to the newest hire — should follow four operating principles.

1

Always invite AI to the table

Use it for everything — drafts, research, brainstorming, analysis — not to get the final answer, but to find where the wall is for your role. The only way to learn the limits is to hit them.

2

Be the human in the loop

AI does the first 80%. A human expert must always verify the final result. AI hallucinates — it confidently states falsehoods. The human's job is not to generate; it is to judge. That judgment requires genuine expertise, which is why AI raises the value of deep human knowledge, not lowers it.

3

Treat it like a person — give it a persona

Assign AI a role: "Act as a skeptical CFO reviewing this business case." or "You are a senior employment lawyer in Singapore — review this policy for risk." This shifts its "headspace" and dramatically improves the quality and specificity of its output. Generic prompts produce generic results.

4

Assume this is the worst version you will ever use

AI capabilities are accelerating exponentially. The model available today is the least capable model you will ever interact with. Do not build legacy processes around today's limitations — build systems that assume the floor keeps rising.

The "Leadership, Lab & Crowd" Model

Slide 5

Controlled AI adoption requires a three-pronged structure. HR must build and sustain all three simultaneously.

🌐

The Crowd

Democratise access. Give everyone the best thinking models — GPT-4, Claude, Gemini. Turn your entire workforce into experimenters. The breakthroughs will come from unexpected places.

🔬

The Lab

A dedicated internal team — even two or three people — whose job is to harvest the breakthroughs found by the Crowd and formalise them into repeatable, scalable processes.

🧭

Leadership

Executives must provide a clear, public vision: AI will be used to expand human capabilities, not simply to cut headcount. Without this signal, the Crowd will hide what they find.

HR's Role: Build All Three at Once

The Crowd without the Lab creates chaos — discoveries disappear. The Lab without the Crowd misses the grassroots intelligence. Leadership without the other two is just a press release. HR is the only function positioned to architect and sustain all three simultaneously.

The Secret Cyborg Problem

Slide 6
The Hidden Risk

Your best people are hiding something

Right now, you have "Secret Cyborgs" — employees using AI to save hours a day but hiding it from managers and colleagues. They fear two things, and both fears are rational.

Fear 1: Punishment

They are using tools that have not been officially sanctioned. In the absence of a clear AI policy, they assume using AI is against the rules — even when it makes their work significantly better.

Fear 2: Downsizing

If they reveal that a day's work now takes one hour, they fear the organisation's response will be to cut their role — not to redirect their newly freed capacity toward higher-value work.

Hard Question
"If your best people are finishing a day's work in an hour — and hiding it from you — what does that tell you about the psychological safety culture you have built?"
HR Policy Action — Radical Rewards

The solution is not surveillance. It is radical incentivisation. Create a programme that rewards employees who come forward with AI automation breakthroughs. Consider:

  • Significant cash bonuses for documented process improvements
  • Promotions or role expansions for AI champions
  • Permanent flexible or remote working arrangements as a reward
  • Public recognition — name their contribution in the company's AI story

Above all, guarantee psychological safety. Announce explicitly: "Using AI to improve your work will never be grounds for discipline or redundancy. We want to know what you have found."

The Snapped Talent Pipeline

Slide 7
Workforce Development

When AI breaks the apprenticeship model

AI has quietly "snapped" the traditional apprenticeship model. Junior employees are using AI to bypass the grunt work — the data entry, the first-draft writing, the basic research — where they used to build their foundational expertise. The result: they can produce outputs that look senior, without having developed the underlying judgment to know when those outputs are wrong.

The Danger

A junior analyst who has never manually built a financial model does not know how to spot a hallucinated figure in an AI-generated model. Competence cannot be shortcut — only the grunt work that builds it can be redesigned.

HR Strategy

Formalised AI Mentorship & Deliberate Practice

1

Formalised AI mentorship programmes

Pair junior employees with senior practitioners who can teach them to critically evaluate AI outputs — not just prompt it, but interrogate its results with the expertise to know when it is wrong.

2

AI simulators for deliberate practice

Use AI to create realistic, high-stakes simulations where juniors must apply their expertise under pressure. Build the judgment separately from the output production.

3

AI as antagonistic coach

Deploy AI in a coaching role — "You are a venture capitalist. Grill this person's pitch until their weakest assumptions collapse." Relentless, personalised feedback at scale. Human experts do the final evaluation; AI does the volume practice.

The Maximalist Strategy

Slide 8
Don't Settle for Incremental

Local Benchmarks & Internal Turing Tests

Document summarisation and email drafting are table stakes. They are not a strategy. A Maximalist AI Strategy means pushing the system until it breaks — deliberately — to establish where the real frontier is for your organisation's specific needs.

  • Push to failure. Assign tasks specifically designed to break the AI. Document where it fails. These failure points become your organisation's local benchmarks — the honest map of what AI can and cannot do for you specifically, not generically.
  • Internal Turing Tests. Have your domain experts judge AI-generated outputs against human outputs — blind. The question is not "is this perfect?" but "is this good enough for our standard?" In many cases, you will be surprised. In some cases, you will not — and that data is equally valuable.
  • Model Agnosticism. Do not bet the organisation on one AI provider. The frontier model changes every few months. Build processes that assume you will switch — and make switching easy. Your workflows should depend on what AI can do, not on which company's AI you are using.

Warning: Incremental Thinking is a Trap

Organisations that use AI only for document summarisation and meeting notes are not becoming AI-native. They are making legacy processes slightly cheaper. The Maximalist question is: what goals that were previously impossible are now within reach?

Reclaiming Agency

Slide 9
"AI is not a steamroller happening to us. We have agency over how it is deployed, at what pace, and in service of what vision."

The Big Path

We can take the "small path" — layoffs, cost-cutting, incremental efficiency gains. Or we can take the Big Path: using the productivity gains that AI unlocks to expand our capabilities and reach goals that were previously impossible. The Guinness model — using efficiency gains to reach new markets, not to reduce headcount. Human taste, curation, and expertise become the most valuable commodities in the Co-Intelligent Enterprise.

Commitment 1 I will identify our organisation's Secret Cyborgs this month — and create a safe channel for them to come forward.
Commitment 2 I will design a Radical Rewards programme that incentivises AI breakthroughs rather than punishing them.
Commitment 3 I will build our Leadership, Lab & Crowd structure — and put HR at the centre of it.

"Human taste, curation, and expertise are the most valuable commodities in the Co-Intelligent Enterprise. AI raises the floor for everyone. HR decides how high the ceiling goes."
