Making Governance Stick

A framework without change management is a policy that lives in a folder. The gap between having a governance structure and people actually using it is entirely a change management problem — and HR is the function best positioned to close it.

15 minutes Training Support structures Champions Gap exercise
The question that opens this segment
If you rolled out your AI governance framework on Monday — with no training, no support channel, and no champions — what percentage of your people would know what to do? And what percentage would quietly go back to using whatever they were already using?
Facilitator note: Do not answer this question. Let it sit for five seconds. Then say: "Governance that nobody uses is not governance. It is documentation. The three levers that close the gap between policy and practice are training, support, and champions. Let us look at each one."

The three change management levers

2:00 – 2:10
1

Training — building the floor

Not a one-off compliance module. A tiered capability programme that meets people where they are.

Four tiers of AI literacy
  • All staff — basic AI awareness and what the governance policy means for their role
  • Practitioners — tool-specific training for teams actively using approved AI
  • Managers — governance literacy, how to apply human-in-the-loop judgement, contextual override
  • Champions — advanced capability, model logic, audit trail, escalation protocols
The DBS model in practice

DBS did not run a compliance briefing. They built 30+ learning roadmaps, an AI-powered personalised learning platform (iGrow), a job shadowing programme (Be My Guest), and a generative AI coaching tool (iCoach) available to every employee. The lesson: training is only as effective as the infrastructure that delivers it at scale.

Hard question
Is your training programme a one-day event or an ongoing capability build?
2

Support structures — removing the friction

Governance fails when the path of least resistance is to do nothing, or to ask nobody. Support structures make compliance the easy option.

Minimum viable support infrastructure
  • A named help desk, Slack channel, or email alias where anyone can ask "is this approved?"
  • A 48-hour sandbox intake process for new tool requests — 5 minutes to submit, decision in 48 hours
  • A monthly governance Q&A — open to all staff, 20 minutes, recurring
  • A clear escalation path for when someone is genuinely unsure and needs a human answer fast
  • A public vetted tools directory — updated quarterly, visible to everyone
Why friction is the enemy of governance

Shadow AI does not happen because people are malicious. It happens because the approved path is slower and harder than the unapproved one. Every piece of support infrastructure you build is reducing the gap between doing the right thing and doing the convenient thing. When those two options become the same option, shadow behaviour stops.

Hard question
If one of your employees wanted to know right now whether a specific AI tool was approved — what would they do? How long would it take? How confident are you they would get the right answer?
3

Champions — the cultural multiplier

The most underused lever. A network of embedded practitioners who model the behaviour, reduce peer anxiety, and surface real-world friction back to HR and IT.

What a champion is — and is not
  • Not an IT person deputised to police tool usage
  • Not an HR person explaining the policy
  • Yes — a frontline practitioner who has used an approved tool, seen it work, and can answer peers' questions from lived experience
  • Yes — someone whose credibility comes from doing the job, not from knowing the policy
  • Ideally one per team or department, nominated by their manager, supported with advanced training
What champions do
  • Answer "is this safe to use?" in real time, in the flow of work
  • Model approved behaviour publicly — make governance visible, not invisible
  • Surface real-world friction — what is not working, what people are confused about, what they are doing instead
  • Celebrate safe innovation within their team — giving governance a positive cultural signal
  • Feed quarterly insights back to HR as part of the continuous audit cycle
Hard question
If you announced your AI governance framework tomorrow, who would you call first to help you land it? That person is your first champion.
DBS tie-back — champions at scale

DBS did not just train 13,000 employees. They built the iGrow platform to personalise the learning journey, the Be My Guest programme to create cross-functional exposure, and iCoach to give every employee access to coaching — not just executives. The implicit champion model at DBS was every manager who went through retraining first and could then model the behaviour for their team. That sequencing — leaders go first, then peers follow — is the cultural multiplier that made the numbers real.

Group exercise — map your change management gaps

2:10 – 2:15
Engagement exercise 4b — Gap assessment  (5 minutes)

Table groups of 3–4. For each of the three levers below, mark your organisation's current state: Strong (in place and working), Weak (exists but not effective), or Missing (does not exist).

Give 3 minutes for individual reflection, then 2 minutes to compare at the table. Debrief question: "Which lever is most consistently missing — and which one would have the highest impact if you fixed it in the next 30 days?"

Lever What good looks like Strong Weak Missing
Training Tiered literacy programme; all staff covered; managers trained separately; ongoing — not a single event ☐ ☐ ☐
Support Named help desk; 48-hour sandbox; monthly Q&A; vetted tools directory publicly accessible ☐ ☐ ☐
Champions Named individual per team; advanced training completed; visible to peers; feeds back to HR quarterly ☐ ☐ ☐
Debrief note: Most tables will mark Training as Weak (something exists), Support as somewhere between Weak and Missing, and Champions as Missing entirely. That pattern is almost universal. Name it: "Champions being missing is not an accident — it requires deliberate investment and a willingness to give frontline practitioners a governance role that HR usually keeps for itself. That shift is where the cultural change actually happens."
